The original *Yakuza* (*Ryu Ga Gotoku*) launched on PlayStation 2 in Japan on December 8, 2005. By any reasonable measure, nobody expected what followed.

Twenty years later, the series has produced over twenty titles, sold more than 23 million copies worldwide, and built one of the most devoted fanbases in gaming. Kazuma Kiryu — a character introduced in a mid-budget PS2 action game about a Tokyo gangster — has become one of the most recognisable figures in the medium.

## How It Started

Director Toshihiro Nagoshi pitched the first Yakuza as a story about the humanity within a world defined by codes, hierarchy, and violence. The game's setting — a meticulous recreation of Kabukicho, Tokyo's red-light district — was unusual at a time when open-world games tended toward either American crime fiction or fantasy. The specific Japanese urban texture was the point.

The original game sold modestly in Japan and struggled in the West, where SEGA's localisation budget was minimal. It didn't seem like the beginning of anything.

## The Turning Point

*Yakuza 0*, released in Japan in 2015 and worldwide in 2017, changed the series' trajectory permanently. A prequel that doubled as a reintroduction to the world, it arrived on Steam at a time when a new audience was ready for it. The viral spread of *Baka Mitai* as a meme in 2020 accelerated things further, bringing the series to players who might never have found it through traditional channels.

## The Numbers

- **23+ million copies** sold across the series as of 2024
- **20+ titles** across mainline, remasters, remakes, and spin-offs  
- Consistent critical reception — every mainline entry since Yakuza 0 has scored above 80 on Metacritic
- Growing Western audience, with *Infinite Wealth* reporting the series' highest Western sales to date

## What's Next

*Pirate Yakuza in Hawaii* arrived in February 2025, the most recent entry. RGG Studio have not confirmed their next project but their output rate — rarely more than a year between releases — suggests an announcement is not far off.

Twenty years in, the studio is making bigger, more expensive, more ambitious games than ever. Not a bad run for a mid-budget PS2 game about a man who refuses to lie down.
