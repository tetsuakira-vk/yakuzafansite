## Series Callbacks and Long-Range References

### Yakuza 0 as a Prequel Written Retroactively
Yakuza 0 was written knowing the full history of Kiryu and Majima through Yakuza 5. This creates a category of easter egg unique to the series — moments that mean nothing to a first-time player but land with full force for returning fans.

The scene where a young Majima is told he's been expelled from the Shimano family — that he no longer exists as a yakuza — mirrors, almost word for word, the moment in later games where he discusses what it means to truly belong to nothing. Yakuza 0 is full of these. The game knows what you know.

### Majima's Eye
Goro Majima wears an eyepatch over his left eye in every game from Yakuza 0 onwards. The story of how he lost the eye is told in Yakuza 0 across his full story arc. Players who reach the end of Majima's storyline understand every subsequent eyepatch appearance as a walking reminder.

### Kiryu's Tattoo
The dragon tattoo on Kiryu's back appears in cutscenes throughout the series. In Yakuza 0, an elderly tattooist begins the work. In Kiwami 2, the tattoo's specific symbolism is referenced in dialogue about what it means to carry the image of a dragon. In Yakuza 6, the tattoo is central to a story moment involving people who've never met Kiryu but know what the image means.

## Developer Presence in the Games

### SEGA Arcade Appearances
SEGA's arcade brand, Club Sega, appears in every mainline entry. Beyond the brand, fully playable SEGA games are embedded throughout — Virtua Fighter, Super Monkey Ball, OutRun, Space Harrier. These are complete, functional ports of their original titles and represent SEGA inserting its entire catalogue history into the games' fiction.

### Don Quijote
The real-world discount chain Don Quijote appears in the games by name, with the actual store interior recreated. RGG Studio secured an agreement for its depiction. The in-game Don Quijote sells actual products at their real approximate price points. It is, essentially, a real shop inside a game.

### Kamoji
A recurring NPC who appears in the background of almost every game. He wears a distinctive yellow tracksuit and has appeared in Kamurocho scenes from Yakuza 1 onwards. Spotting Kamoji is a running series tradition among long-term players.

## Substory Self-Reference

### The Video Store Clerk — *Yakuza 0*
In Yakuza 0, a substory involves helping a video rental store clerk deal with a customer dispute. The resolution involves the clerk's encyclopaedic knowledge of film. At one point he references a film about a yakuza who lives by a code and cannot escape his past. This is not an accident.

### Pocket Circuit Fighter's Hidden Name
The enthusiastic Pocket Circuit organiser in Yakuza 0 is named Pocket Circuit Fighter in the credits. He appears in substories in later games. His backstory, revealed across a chain of optional content, implies a more complicated life than his hobby suggests.

## Real Location Details

### The Batting Centre Sign
The entrance sign above the batting centre in Kamurocho has changed slightly between games to reflect the fictional passage of time and business ownership changes. It's background detail that nobody asked for and is entirely consistent across the entire series.

### Convenience Store Inventory
The convenience stores in Kamurocho stock fictional equivalents of real Japanese convenience store products — energy drinks, onigiri, cup noodles — with product names slightly altered for licensing reasons but package design clearly modelled on real products. The inventory changes slightly per game era, reflecting real convenience store product evolution.

## The Tachibana Real Estate Logo
In Yakuza 0, the Tachibana Real Estate company logo is a stylised crane. The crane appears in Kamurocho signage across the series' timeline — the company no longer exists by the time of the later games, but the sign does, aging and faded in the background.

## Combat System Easter Eggs

### Komaki Style Master
The elderly martial arts master Sotaro Komaki appears in Yakuza 0 and teaches Kiryu advanced combat techniques. He reappears in Yakuza 4 and Yakuza 5, still teaching, apparently ageless. His role as the series' eternal combat instructor is never commented upon.

### The Dragon of Dojima Title
Kiryu's title — the Dragon of Dojima — is given to him by antagonists and allies alike, always as a measure of fear or respect. In Yakuza 0, no one uses it. In Kiwami, it's used twice. By Yakuza 5, its use is casual shorthand for an entire category of threat. Tracking its first appearances and contexts across the series is a minor but satisfying exercise.

## Development Trivia

### The Dragon Engine's Name
The Dragon Engine, introduced in Kiwami 2, is named partly for Kiryu's Dragon of Dojima title and partly as an internal reference to the scale of the rebuild. It processes facial expressions using motion capture of the actors providing voice performances — a technique the series has used since Yakuza 6 and which is responsible for the quality of emotional delivery in the later games.

### Voice Cast Continuity
Kazuma Kiryu has been voiced by Takaya Kuroda in every mainline appearance across twenty years. Goro Majima has been voiced by Hidenari Ugaki across the same period. The continuity of the Japanese voice cast is unusual in gaming and is part of what gives the characters their accumulated weight. When Kiryu sounds tired in Infinite Wealth, it's the same voice that sounded hopeful in Yakuza 0, and the actor has had twenty years of material to draw on.

### The Localization Change
The English title "Yakuza" was chosen by SEGA of America for the 2006 Western release and was used for sixteen years before the rebrand to "Like a Dragon" in 2022. The rebrand matched the original Japanese title *Ryu Ga Gotoku* ("Like a Dragon" / "As If A Dragon") and was intended to signal the series' tonal evolution post-Kiryu. The reaction from Western fans ranged from bemused to frustrated. The games themselves are unaffected.
