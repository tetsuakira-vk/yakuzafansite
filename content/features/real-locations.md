## Kamurocho → Kabukicho, Shinjuku, Tokyo

The series' home city — dense, neon-lit, full of hostess clubs, convenience stores, and alleyways — is based on **Kabukicho**, Tokyo's largest red-light and entertainment district, located in the Shinjuku ward.

The resemblance is deliberate and meticulous. RGG Studio maps specific streets, building layouts, and landmarks from real Kabukicho into Kamurocho. Several real businesses have appeared in-game (with permission), and the topology — the grid structure, the central boulevard, the back alleys — matches the real location closely enough that you can use the game as a rough mental map.

**What's real:**
- The central boulevard (modelled on Kabukicho Ichiban-gai / the main entertainment strip)
- The park (Miyashita Park area)
- The general density and visual language of hostess clubs, pachinko parlours, and convenience stores

**What's changed:**
- Scale. Kamurocho is compressed. The real Kabukicho covers a much larger area.
- Specific buildings are composite or invented.
- The Don Quijote (rendered as Don Quijote in the games) appears as is — with SEGA's approval.

**If you visit:** Walk north from Shinjuku Station's east exit. The main Kabukicho gate (Ichiban-gai entrance) is instantly recognisable to series veterans.

## Sotenbori → Dotonbori, Osaka

Kiryu and Majima's second city is based on **Dotonbori**, Osaka's canal-side entertainment district.

The Sotenbori River maps directly to the Dotonbori River, and the bridge layout is recognisable from the games. The canal-side restaurant strip, the neon density, and the general atmosphere of Osaka's more chaotic, louder energy compared to Tokyo are all present.

**What's real:**
- The Sotenbori River / Dotonbori River canal layout
- The general visual identity — massive signage, restaurant facades, bridge crossings
- The Glico Running Man sign (rendered as a fictional equivalent in the games)

**What's changed:**
- The Kani Doraku giant crab sign (Sotenbori has a version)
- Some specific shops are composite or relocated

**If you visit:** Namba Station → walk north toward the Dotonbori canal. The famous Glico sign is immediately visible from the main bridge.

## Ijincho → Isezaki-cho, Yokohama

The setting of the Ichiban era's first game — a large, multicultural port city district — is based on **Isezaki-cho** (Isezakicho), a commercial and entertainment district in Yokohama's Naka ward.

Yokohama is Japan's second-largest city and its historical status as a port open to foreign trade gives it a different cultural mix from Tokyo or Osaka. The game captures this — Ijincho has Chinatown, a distinct ethnic neighbourhood in the city's south, and the overall feel of a port district that's seen better decades.

**What's real:**
- Yokohama Chinatown (one of the largest in the world, depicted in-game)
- The overall street layout and commercial character of Isezakicho
- The general multicultural atmosphere

## Ryukyu → Naha, Okinawa

Yakuza 3's second setting is based on **Kokusai-dori** (International Street) in Naha, Okinawa's capital.

Okinawa's distinct identity — historically a separate kingdom, culturally different from mainland Japan, with strong US military presence — is present throughout Yakuza 3. The coastal light, the architecture, and the pace of life are accurately rendered.

**What's real:**
- The shopping street layout (Kokusai-dori is Okinawa's main tourist street)
- The distinctly non-Tokyo atmosphere
- The ocean proximity

## Onomichi → Onomichi, Hiroshima Prefecture

Yakuza 6's second city is based directly on **Onomichi**, a small port city in Hiroshima Prefecture — and unlike the larger city recreations, it's remarkably accurate.

Onomichi is known for its hillside temples, narrow paths, and its position on the Seto Inland Sea. The game renders the specific geography — the hill with temple paths, the cable car, the shoreline, the small-town commercial district — with enough fidelity that visitors report a strong sense of recognition.

**If you visit:** Onomichi is accessible from Hiroshima by train in about an hour. The hill temple walk (Senko-ji Park) is the most immediately recognisable location from Yakuza 6.

## Honolulu → Honolulu, Hawaii

Like a Dragon: Infinite Wealth sets its main portion in a version of Honolulu that is simultaneously looser in its mapping and more ambitious in its scope than the Japanese city recreations.

The game captures the visual and cultural identity of Honolulu — the beach strips, the resort hotels, the multicultural population, the contrast between tourist Waikiki and the working-class districts further from the beach — without adhering to specific street layouts.

## The RGG Studio Research Approach

RGG Studio sends location scouts to every city before building it. This is a well-documented part of their development process — staff photograph streets, eat in local restaurants, and document the specific texture of each location's commercial life before modelling it. The series' cities feel inhabited because they are modelled on places that are inhabited. The Kamurocho convenience stores sell specific products that real Japanese convenience stores sell. The arcades have the specific lighting of real Japanese arcades.

This commitment to location fidelity is one of the less-celebrated aspects of the series' design and one of the most responsible for its atmosphere.
