> Substory descriptions are kept vague to avoid spoiling the experience. That said — mild setup spoilers follow.

Substories are the soul of the series. The main story deals in yakuza politics, honour, and tragedy. Substories are where the game exhales — a comedian trying to find his confidence, a man whose cat has become the neighbourhood's most respected figure, a child testing whether a stranger is genuinely kind. These are some of the best.

---

## Yakuza 0

### The Mentor
A veteran comedian takes Kiryu under his wing and asks him to become a straight man for a comedy duo. The substory chain runs across several encounters and ends with a live performance. The writing here is warm and the payoff lands entirely on character work.

### The Dragon & Tiger
Kurosawa, a real estate agent, hires Kiryu to help him acquire properties through unorthodox means. The escalating situations he gets into constitute an entire comedy arc within the larger game.

### The Homeless Man's Legacy
Found in a park, this substory starts as a simple errand and quietly reveals something unexpectedly moving about the person making the request. One of several Y0 substories that uses the setting — 1988, a Japan of extreme wealth and forgotten margins — to say something about who gets left behind.

### Phone Club
Kiryu can subscribe to a phone club service and have extended conversations with women across Tokyo. The substories triggered by these conversations range from heartwarming to genuinely strange. Several run to multiple chapters. The full arc of one character in particular is surprisingly well-written.

---

## Yakuza Kiwami

### A Shining Example
The Majima Everywhere system generates some of the series' funniest moments, but the best substory involving Majima in Kiwami is one where he's in a role he was clearly not built for. Watching Majima attempt sincerity is deeply funny and oddly touching.

---

## Yakuza Kiwami 2

### The Bouncer's Pride
A bouncer at a hostess club has built a reputation over years of working the door. Someone threatens to take that from him. Kiryu gets involved. The substory is short but has real weight — it takes the profession seriously.

---

## Yakuza 3

### The Orphanage Substories (All of Them)
Yakuza 3 has a substory for each child at Morning Glory Orphanage, and taken together they constitute the emotional argument for why Kiryu's retirement matters. Some are funny. One will ruin you. They're all worth it.

---

## Yakuza 4

### The Debt Collector
Akiyama's substory involving a debt he's been asked to collect from an unexpected source. The Sky Finance business model — loans to people who prove themselves worthy — gets its clearest articulation here.

---

## Yakuza 5

### The Taxi Passenger Series
Kiryu's cover in Yakuza 5 is as a taxi driver. Several substories are triggered by specific passengers. One of these — a man who takes one particular fare — is one of the series' finest pieces of writing in 25 hours of side content.

### Haruka's Idol Substories
Haruka's idol storyline generates substories about the music industry, fan culture, and performance anxiety. They're tonally unlike anything else in the series and most of them are excellent.

---

## Yakuza 6

### Onomichi Locals
Yakuza 6's second city, Onomichi, is a small coastal town in Hiroshima Prefecture. The substories here feel quieter and more grounded than the Kamurocho content — people with small, specific problems that a man like Kiryu is comically over-qualified to solve.

---

## Yakuza: Like a Dragon

### The Visionary
A man with an unusual talent who needs Ichiban's help accessing it. The setup is bizarre. The resolution is genuinely funny. Ichiban's enthusiasm — his tendency to read heroism into ordinary situations — is at its most endearing.

### Friends and Family
The party member substories across Like a Dragon are among the best the series has done. Each party member gets a chain of substories that develops their backstory and relationship with Ichiban. Nanba's chain in particular is exceptional.

---

## Like a Dragon: Infinite Wealth

### The Sujimon Research Chain
The creature-collecting Sujimon system generates its own substory arc involving a researcher who has perhaps taken their work too seriously. It's a commentary on obsessive fandom told entirely in the idiom of the thing it's commenting on.

### Kiryu's Bucket List
Kiryu, dealing with a terminal diagnosis, maintains a list of things he wants to experience before the end. The substories attached to this list are among the most emotionally demanding in the series. Several of them circle back to characters and moments from games you may have played fifteen years ago.

---

## What Makes a Great Substory?

The best substories in the series share a few qualities:
- **They take the subject seriously, however absurd.** Even the silliest substories treat the person asking for help with dignity.
- **They're self-contained.** A substory that requires ten hours of other substories to understand has failed.
- **They use the protagonist correctly.** Kiryu being dragged into something he's wildly overqualified for. Ichiban finding the heroic narrative in something mundane.
- **They end.** A substory that resolves cleanly — even if the resolution is ambiguous — is worth more than one that gestures at closure without achieving it.
