## Yakuza 0

### Shibusawa — Final Boss
The final confrontation in Yakuza 0 is a clean, well-telegraphed brawl that lets Kiryu use everything the game has taught him. Where the story's antagonism has been political and systemic, this fight brings it back to fists in a parking garage. Satisfying in a way that's hard to articulate.

### The Kuze Series
Hiroki Kuze is a recurring boss you fight four times. Each rematch is escalated — he breaks his hands, ties them together, escalates his desperation. By the fourth fight the emotional weight has been built across the entire game. One of the best recurring-boss structures in the series.

### Majima's Final Confrontation
Majima's Sotenbori storyline culminates in a fight that feels earned across fifteen hours of his arc. Context does all the work — you know exactly why this fight means what it does.

## Yakuza Kiwami

### Nishikiyama — Final Boss
You've spent the entire game watching Akira Nishikiyama deteriorate from the generous friend of Yakuza 0 into something corroded by jealousy and circumstance. The final fight is less a challenge and more a statement. Bring tissues.

### Goro Majima — Every Single Time
Kiwami introduces the Majima Everywhere system where Majima ambushes Kiryu across the city in increasingly elaborate disguises. A chef. A zombie. A traffic cone. Every ambush is a fight and every fight is slightly absurd. The system exists to rebuild Kiryu's combat skills after a decade in prison, but the real reason is that more Majima is always the correct design decision.

## Yakuza Kiwami 2

### Ryuji Goda — Multiple Rounds
The Dragon of Kansai is one of the series' most compelling antagonists, and the game gives him multiple encounters that build toward a finale staged on a burning rooftop. The last fight between Kiryu and Goda is the series at its most operatic.

---

## Yakuza 3

### Mine — Final Boss
Yoshitaka Mine's motivation is revealed slowly across Yakuza 3 and the final fight carries an emotional charge that's unusual for an antagonist. The setting — a rooftop in a storm — does what the best boss arenas do: makes the geography feel like punctuation.

---

## Yakuza 4

### All Four Finales
Yakuza 4's structure gives each of its four protagonists their own climactic confrontation. Akiyama's final fight is the flashiest. Saejima's is the most brutal. Tanimura's is built on a 25-year grudge. Kiryu's brings the threads together. The weakest of the four is still a strong fight.

---

## Yakuza 5

### Kiryu vs Aizawa
Aizawa is built up across Yakuza 5 as an almost supernatural threat, and when the fight arrives the game justifies the hype. One of the more technically demanding fights in the series.

### Saejima vs Baba
Taiga Saejima's Yakuza 5 arc builds toward a confrontation with another fighter who shares his background. The fight has a weight to it that pure action games rarely achieve.

## Like a Dragon: Ishin!

### Okita Souji — Recurring Rival
The historical swordsman Okita Souji serves as Ryoma's primary rival across Ishin!. Their encounters escalate in complexity and the final fight is the best sword-combat exchange in the series.

## Like a Dragon: Infinite Wealth

### Bryce Fairchild — Penultimate
Infinite Wealth's villain has one of the more memorable designs in the series and the encounter with him lands because the game takes its time building what he represents. A fight that pays off 80+ hours of setup.

## General Tips for Hard Fights

- **Heat Actions exist for a reason.** If a fight is draining your health, you're probably not using environmental Heat Actions enough.
- **Save before major boss encounters.** Not all games autosave at the right moment.
- **For turn-based fights (Ichiban era):** status effects matter. Don't ignore them.
- **Dodge timing:** most bosses have a distinct attack animation before the strike. Learn it.
- **Items are not shameful.** Hard fights are hard by design, not to test your resolve to die repeatedly.
