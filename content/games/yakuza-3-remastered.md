## Story

Kazuma Kiryu has left the yakuza life behind. He runs the **Morning Glory Orphanage** in Okinawa, raising a group of children including Haruka Sawamura. It is the quietest, most human version of Kiryu the series has given us — and it doesn't last.

A government resort development project threatens the orphanage's land. Behind it, a shadow conspiracy reaches into the highest levels of the Tojo Clan and beyond. Kiryu is pulled back to Tokyo, then back to Okinawa, fighting on two fronts to protect his family.

Yakuza 3 is slower and more domestic than its predecessors by design. The orphanage sequences — helping kids with their problems, running on the beach, just living — make the violence of the yakuza world feel heavier by contrast.

---

## New Setting: Ryukyu (Okinawa)

The introduction of a second explorable city beyond Kamurocho opened up the series considerably. Ryukyu is smaller but distinctly characterised — beach town atmosphere, local cuisine, and a cast of Okinawan characters with their own dynamics.

---

## Remaster Notes

The Western remaster restored content that was cut from the original 2010 Western release, including several substories and the hostess club minigame. If you played the original Western version, the remaster is the definitive version.

---

## Why It Matters

Yakuza 3 is the game that fully established what the series is emotionally. Kiryu as a father figure, protecting children from an indifferent world, is the beating heart underneath all the street brawls. Missing this game means missing why *Yakuza 6* lands as hard as it does.
