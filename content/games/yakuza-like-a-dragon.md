## Story

Ichiban Kasuga is a low-ranking yakuza who goes to prison for eighteen years on behalf of his patriarch — an act of loyalty he considers the most honourable thing he's ever done. When he walks out, his patriarch shoots him and leaves him for dead in the ruins of Yokohama's Isezaki Ijincho district.

Ichiban has nothing: no family, no clan, no future. What he has is Dragon Quest, the JRPG series he grew up on, and the unshakeable belief that if he acts like a hero in a story, a hero is what he'll become.

What follows is a classic JRPG structure — ragtag party, small victories, building toward an enemy who turns out to be more complicated than expected — wrapped in RGG Studio's signature texture. The streets of Ijincho are as alive as Kamurocho. The side content is as absurd and as human as anything in the Kiryu saga.

## Turn-Based Combat

The switch to turn-based RPG was the most controversial decision in the series' history and one of its best. Ichiban's Dragon Quest obsession is the in-universe explanation — he literally visualises his street fights as JRPG battles. The system is:

- **Jobs** — characters switch between dozens of job classes (Bodyguard, Host, Musician, Chef, Night Queen, etc.), each with different stats, abilities, and skills
- **Poundmates** — summon special assist characters (local celebrities, substory friends, business contacts) for powerful attacks
- **Positional play** — position matters; enemies can be knocked into each other or off hazards

The combat is deep, surprisingly strategic, and full of jokes that land because the game is fully committed to the bit.

## The Party

Ichiban's companions are the richest supporting cast in the series:
- **Yu Nanba** — a homeless former nurse with nothing left to lose
- **Koichi Adachi** — a disgraced ex-detective with a grudge to settle
- **Joon-gi Han** — a Korean yakuza with a complicated dual identity
- **Tianyou Zhao** — a Triads leader playing a longer game than anyone realises

## Why Start Here?

*Yakuza: Like a Dragon* is genuinely the second-best entry point in the series after *Yakuza 0*. It is largely self-contained, introduces all its systems from scratch, and its protagonist is as loveable to new players as he is to veterans. Kiryu appears but as a supporting figure.
